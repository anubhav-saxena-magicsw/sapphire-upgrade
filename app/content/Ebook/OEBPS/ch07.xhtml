1. Measure and value inventories.

IAS 2 Inventories

1.1 Measurement of inventories

Having seen the importance of inventories to the final accounts, the next questions that arise are (i) how do we ensure that the amounts disclosed are true and fair; and (ii) how do we measure them, i.e. how do we determine the values to be recognised in the financial statements?

Definition

Inventories shall be measured at the lower of cost and net realisable value.

There is a need to understand how to calculate cost and net realisable value.

Cost of inventories

This includes:

1. Costs of purchase

2. Costs of conversions

3. Other costs incurred in bringing the inventories to their present location and condition

1. Costs of purchase include the following:

a) Purchase price

b) Import duties and other taxes, excluding those subsequently recoverable from taxation authorities

c) Transport, handling and other costs directly attributable to the acquisition of materials, finished goods and services

Trade discounts / rebates are deducted, if not already deducted from the purchase price.

Example

Saliburtan Ltd purchased raw materials for $125,000 less a rebate of 2%. It paid $25,000 as import duty, including $10,000 towards a special duty. According to local tax laws, it will get a credit of the amount paid towards the special duty, while determining its customs liability. It spent $3,000 on ocean freight, clearing agent’s charges of $2,000, $4,000 on warehouse rent and $1,500 on the security guard’s salary.

2. Costs of conversion

Conversion refers to the change in condition of the material into a finished or saleable product. Conversion costs can be discussed under two broad headings:

a) Costs directly related to the units of production e.g. a worker spent 6 hours on completing the item; the wages paid to the worker for the time can be directly related to the units of production.

b) Allocation of production overheads: fixed or variable

Fixed production overheads

i. Allocation is based on normal capacity.

ii. Normal capacity is the expected average over a number of periods, after taking into account the loss of capacity due to normal maintenance.

iii. If there is low production, the overhead allocated to each unit is not increased.

iv. Unallocated overheads are recognised as an expense in the period in which they are incurred.

Variable production overheads

Allocation of these overheads is based on the actual use of the production facilities.

Example

At Benjini’s Restaurant, the fuel consumed for cooking is a variable overhead (indirect material). For every 100 meals, Benjini needs fuel of $5.

Therefore, per meal overhead cost is 5 cents. Benjini uses raw material of $1 and pays wages of 50 cents per unit.

The total variable cost (excluding absorption of fixed overheads) of the product is $1.00 raw material + 0.50 wages + 0.05 variable overheads = $1.55.

Example

Bojour Ltd incurs a fixed production of $1,000,000 every year. Its normal capacity of production is 100,000 units. In the year 2006, 80,000 units were produced and in 2007, 120,000 units were produced.

In 2006 the actual production is short of normal capacity and therefore production overhead will be allocated on basis of normal capacity. Therefore fixed production overhead will be $1,000,000 / 100,000 units = $10 per unit.

In 2007 the actual production is more than the normal capacity and therefore production overhead will be allocated on basis of actual production. Therefore fixed production overhead will be $1,000,000/120,000 units = $8.33 per unit.

3. Other costs

Other costs are included in the cost of inventories to the extent that they are incurred for bringing the inventories to their present location and condition.

Example

A product is manufactured according to the specifications given by the customer. Material cost is $300,000 and wages are $130,000. Professional fees of $25,000 are paid for having the product designed.

The fees of $25,000, being ‘other’ costs, are to be added to the cost of the product manufactured. The total costs are:

$300,000 +$130,000 + $25,000 = $455,000

4. Costs excluded from the cost of inventory

Costs that do not satisfy the above conditions are not included, but are recognised as expenses in the period in which they are incurred. Some instances are:

a) Abnormal costs such as abnormal wastage of material, labour, and other costs e.g. loss of material by fire, idle time of workers due to machine breakdown.

b) Storage costs except those that are necessary in the production process before the further production stage.

Example

Rent for a warehouse where goods are kept is not included in cost of inventory.

However, in a brewery, if a particular alcohol is to be matured by storing it in a special container for a specific period of time, then the cost of that storage may be added to the cost of inventory.

c) Administrative overheads, except those which are incurred for bringing the inventories to their present location and condition. For example salaries of office staff are not added to the cost of inventory, but salaries of the production accountant can be added, as he is needed for production to occur.

d) Selling costs: for example commission paid to sales and marketing people should not add to the cost of inventory.

e) Borrowing costs, except to the extent permitted by IAS 23 Borrowing Costs.

1.2 Cost formulae

A cost formula is needed to determine the value of material issued and material remaining in inventory. The selection of the most appropriate cost formula depends upon the nature of the items.

1. Specific identification method (for the items not ordinarily interchangeable)

This method is applied to items that are not ordinarily interchangeable and goods and services produced and segregated for specific projects. Specific costs are attributed to the items of inventory.

Example

For special made-to-order furniture, the specific costs of material and labour used therein are identified and included in the cost.

2. Other methods allowable under IAS 2 (for items ordinarily interchangeable)

Either of the following two methods is to be used for items not covered under the specific identification method above. This means that the two methods are for ordinarily interchangeable goods and services.

a) First In First Out (FIFO)

This method assumes that the item which came first into inventory (by way of purchase or production) went out first (as sales or consumption). This means that the items remaining in hand are those which were purchased the last. This is an assumption for the purpose of determining the cost, and the actual movement of the goods may or may not be in that manner. However, generally, the storekeepers ensure that the oldest items are moved out first.

b) Weighted Average

Total cost of material (opening inventory plus subsequent purchases) is divided by the total quantity (opening inventory plus subsequent purchases). This is done either after every purchase or periodically.

It should be noted that the Last In, First Out method (LIFO) is not permitted by IAS 2.

1.3 Net realisable value

Example

Net realisable value is the estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business, less estimated costs of completion and estimated costs necessary to make the sale.

Costs are compared with the realisable value, since the inventories are to be measured at the lower of the two. This is based on the view that assets should not be carried at a value which is in excess of their realisable value.

1. Net realisable values are determined after taking into consideration:

a) The most reliable estimates available.

b) Fluctuations after the end of the reporting period, to the extent that they confirm the conditions existing on the end of the reporting period. (Remember IAS 10 events after the end of the reporting period )

c) The purpose for which the inventory is held.

Example

Bolton Inc specifically manufactured an item for a firm sales order, with a cost of $90,000 to date. The order value is $100,000. Costs to completion are expected to be $14,000.

Net realisable value is $100,000 – $14,000 = $86,000. Cost to date is $90,000.

A write-down is required of $90,000 – $86,000 = $4,000.

2. Raw materials and other supplies held for use in production need not be written below cost if the finished goods in which they are going to be used are likely to fetch a price higher than the total cost.

3. A fresh assessment is made in each subsequent period. If the situation has changed, the amount of the write-down may be reversed. The new carrying amount will be the lower of the new net realisable value and the new cost.

Example

Continuing with the above example of Bolton

In the next period, if the item is fully completed with a cost of $101,000 and is still in hand (against a contract price of $100,000), the expected loss and required write-down is only $1,000. However, $4,000 has already been written down. The excess of $3,000 can be written back to the statement of profit or loss.

1.4 Recognition as an expense

Inventory may be recognised as an expense when:

1. Inventory is sold. Its carrying amount is recognised as an expense

2. Write-downs to net realisable value are needed (as discussed above)

Example

If the carrying value is $6,120 and the NRV is $6,092, the entity has to recognise $28 as an expense in the statement of profit or loss.

3. Losses of inventory e.g. loss by fire or theft

Quick Quiz

1. An entity paid $100,000 as a purchase price and $2,500 as freight for the material. It incurred $3,000 as a salary to the warehouse keeper. What is the cost of inventory?

2. A company manufactured special equipment according to the specifications given by the customer. There is no other equipment similar to this. Out of FIFO and Weighted Average, which cost formula will be used to determine the cost of this inventory item?

3. An item of raw material inventory costing $10,000 has an NRV of $9,500. The finished goods in which the item is to be used is expected to have a cost of $25,000 and an NRV of $27,000. What should be the write-down of the raw material item?

Answers to Quick Quiz

1. The cost of inventory is $102,500. Salary of the warehouse keeper is a storage cost not to be added.

2. Since it is an item not ordinarily interchangeable, the specific identification method should be used. (FIFO and Weighted Average are not relevant.)

3. Since the finished goods in which the raw material is to be used has an NRV higher than cost, no write-down is required.
